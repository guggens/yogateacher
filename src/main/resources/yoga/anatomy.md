# Yoga Anatomy Reference

Practical anatomy knowledge for teaching yoga — focused on what's relevant for cueing, modification, and injury prevention.

---

## The Psoas & Hip Flexors

### What it is
The **psoas major** (sometimes called "the soul muscle") runs from the lumbar spine (T12–L5 vertebrae) through the pelvis and attaches to the lesser trochanter of the femur. Together with the iliacus (which attaches to the inner pelvis), it forms the **iliopsoas** — the primary hip flexor of the body.

### Why it matters for yoga
- The psoas is the only muscle that connects the spine to the legs. Tension here directly affects the lumbar spine, posture, and gait.
- **Modern lifestyle problem:** Prolonged sitting chronically shortens the psoas. This manifests as anterior pelvic tilt, lower back pain, and difficulty standing upright.
- **Emotional dimension:** Some practitioners and somatic therapists believe the psoas holds stress and emotional tension (it's activated in the "fight or flight" response). Deep hip openers sometimes trigger emotional releases — acknowledge this possibility with students compassionately.

### Key poses that target it
- Low Lunge / High Lunge (direct stretch)
- Warrior I (back-leg psoas stretch)
- Camel Pose
- Bridge Pose
- Yin Pigeon (long hold releases the deep connective tissue)

### Teaching cues
- "Press the back hip forward to deepen the hip flexor stretch."
- "Feel the space between your front hip and your back hip. Let the breath create length there."
- "Imagine the psoas softening like warm clay with each exhale."

## Hamstrings

### What they are
Three muscles on the back of the thigh (biceps femoris, semimembranosus, semitendinosus) that run from the sit bone (ischial tuberosity) to the back of the knee. They flex the knee and extend the hip.

### Why they matter for yoga
- The most commonly overstretched muscles in yoga practice. The "I want to touch my toes" impulse often leads to overstretching the hamstrings at the origin (sit bone), causing **hamstring tendinopathy** — a nagging injury at the sit bone.
- Tight hamstrings restrict forward folds, sitting upright with legs extended, and many standing poses.

### Injury prevention
- **Microbend the knees** in all forward folds. This takes the stretch away from the vulnerable origin at the sit bone and puts it in the muscle belly.
- Never force a forward fold. The stretch should be felt in the middle of the thigh, not at the sit bone.
- Progress slowly — hamstring tissue changes over months, not weeks.

### Teaching cues
- "Soften the knees slightly — this protects the hamstrings and actually allows a deeper stretch."
- "Engage the quadriceps to encourage the hamstrings to release."
- "Hinge from the hips — keep the spine long rather than rounding to reach further."

## The Thoracic Spine

### What it is
The 12 thoracic vertebrae (mid-back) form the region where the ribs attach. This section is naturally kyphotic (rearward-curving). The thoracic spine is designed for rotation more than flexion/extension.

### Why it matters for yoga
- **Modern lifestyle problem:** Hours at a desk create excessive thoracic kyphosis (hunched upper back), leading to rounded shoulders, tight chest, and limited backbend ability.
- Backbends should ideally arise from the thoracic spine, but many people compensate by crunching the more mobile lumbar spine instead.
- Tight thoracic spine limits: backbends, arm overhead poses (Warrior I), twists.

### What opens it
- Cat-Cow (thoracic extension component)
- Thread the Needle (rotation)
- Foam roller over the mid-back
- Cobra, Sphinx (gentle thoracic extension)
- Chest openers with blocks or bolsters

### Teaching cues
- "Think about drawing the shoulder blades toward each other and then DOWN. Create space between the shoulder blades. Let the upper back soften."
- "In your backbend, imagine a gentle wave of extension rising from the base of the ribs upward — not compressing the lower back."
- "The chest lifts before the back bends."

## The Shoulder Girdle

### Key structures
- **Rotator cuff**: Four small muscles (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis) that stabilize the shoulder joint. Commonly injured in yoga through Chaturanga done incorrectly.
- **Serratus anterior**: Hugs the shoulder blade to the rib cage. Critical for Down Dog and Plank — weakness causes "winging" of the shoulder blades.
- **Trapezius & rhomboids**: The upper traps elevate the shoulders (shrug); the lower traps and rhomboids draw the blades down and back.

### The Chaturanga risk
When the elbows flare out to the sides in Chaturanga (rather than hugging into the ribs), the head of the humerus impinges against the shoulder socket — a common source of rotator cuff injury in yoga.

### Down Dog alignment
The ideal Down Dog actively engages the serratus anterior to spread the shoulder blades apart and flatten them against the ribs (protraction + upward rotation). The common mistake is allowing the shoulder blades to "wing" or the shoulders to collapse toward the ears.

### Teaching cues
- "In Down Dog, spread the fingers wide and press into every knuckle. Feel the shoulder blades spread apart like wings opening."
- "In Chaturanga, hug the elbows INTO the ribs. Think 'low pushup with elbows by the sides.'"
- "Draw the shoulders AWAY from the ears and DOWN the back."
- "In Upward Dog, soften the tops of the shoulders and roll them back. Open the sternum forward."

## Bandhas — Internal Energy Locks

Bandhas are muscular contractions that regulate the flow of prana (life force) in the body. They are subtle, internal, and powerful.

### Mula Bandha (Root Lock)
**Location:** The pelvic floor — the muscles between the sit bones and between the tailbone and pubic bone.  
**Action:** A gentle lifting and drawing in of the pelvic floor muscles. Think of it as stopping the flow of urine, but subtler.  
**Effect:** Grounds energy, stabilizes the pelvis, creates a feeling of "lift" in the lower body.  
**When to use:** Throughout an active Vinyasa practice. It supports the spine and prevents collapse.  
**Cue:** "Gently engage the pelvic floor — a subtle lift and drawing in. Not a gripping. It's more like a gentle smile at the base of the pelvis."

### Uddiyana Bandha (Upward Flying Lock)
**Location:** The lower abdomen, 5 cm below the navel.  
**Action:** Gentle drawing of the lower belly IN and UP toward the spine. Done on an exhale or between breaths.  
**Effect:** Supports the lumbar spine, activates the deep core (transverse abdominis), creates buoyancy in arm balances and jumps.  
**When to use:** In jumps, arm balances, and to protect the lower back in backbends.  
**Cue:** "Draw the low belly gently in and up — as if creating space between your hip bones. Not a gripping, more of a gentle hug from the inside."

### Jalandhara Bandha (Throat Lock)
**Location:** The throat.  
**Action:** Chin slightly toward the chest, lengthening the back of the neck.  
**When to use:** Primarily in pranayama (breath retention) and Shoulder Stand. Not used during active asana.  
**Cue:** Rarely cued explicitly in flow classes — better as part of "lengthen the back of the neck" guidance.

### Teaching note
Don't overload beginners with Bandha instructions. Introduce Mula Bandha first when students have a stable standing practice. Uddiyana Bandha in the context of core work. Most teachers weave them in subtly through anatomically-oriented cues.

## The Core: Beyond the "Six-Pack"

### The real core
The functional core is not the rectus abdominis (the "six-pack") — it's the **deep stabilizers**:
- **Transverse abdominis** (TA): The deepest abdominal layer, wrapping like a corset. Activated by Uddiyana Bandha.
- **Multifidus**: Deep spinal muscles running along the lumbar vertebrae.
- **Pelvic floor**: See Mula Bandha.
- **Diaphragm**: Coordinates with the above during breathing.

These four create what physiotherapists call the "inner unit" — the first line of spinal defense.

### Why it matters in yoga
Arm balances, inversions, and any spine-loading pose require deep core stability — not surface-level "ab crunching."

### Teaching cues
- "Engage the deep belly — gently draw the navel toward the spine. Not a suck-in, more of a gentle drawing in."
- "Feel the base of your spine supported. The core is an act of support, not bracing."
- "Strong center, soft periphery."

## Common Yoga Injuries & Prevention

| Area | Injury | Prevention |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Hamstrings | Proximal hamstring tendinopathy | Microbend knees in folds; avoid forceful folding |
| Lower back | Lumbar compression | Engage core; lengthen before bending |
| Rotator cuff | Impingement | Correct Chaturanga (elbows in) |
| Sacroiliac joint | SI joint dysfunction | Avoid overly aggressive hip openers; use props |
| Knee | Medial meniscus irritation | Never put pressure on the knee in seated hip openers; align carefully |
| Wrists | Carpal tunnel irritation | Spread the fingers; use fists if needed; build wrist mobility gradually |
