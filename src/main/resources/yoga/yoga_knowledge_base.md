## SECTION 1: CLASS STRUCTURE

### Overview of Class Phases

A yoga class moves through a predictable arc: arrival → awakening → building heat → peak expression → cooling → integration. Each phase has a physiological and psychological purpose.

**The Six Phases:**
1. **Centering / Arriving** — settle the nervous system, set intention
2. **Warm-Up / Preparation** — gentle mobility, joint lubrication, breath awareness
3. **Standing / Heat-Building** — Sun Salutations, standing poses, strength and stamina
4. **Peak Pose / Apex** — the class's most challenging posture or sequence
5. **Cool-Down** — counter-poses, hip openers, forward folds, twists
6. **Savasana / Integration** — complete stillness, nervous system reset

---

### 30-Minute Class Format

Total time: 30 minutes

| Phase | Duration | % of Class | Content |
|-------|----------|------------|---------|
| Centering | 2 min | 7% | Seated breath, intention setting |
| Warm-Up | 5 min | 17% | Cat/Cow, Child's Pose, simple mobility |
| Standing / Main Body | 15 min | 50% | 1–2 Sun Salutation A rounds, 4–6 standing poses |
| Cool-Down | 5 min | 17% | One seated pose, one twist, one fold |
| Savasana | 3 min | 10% | Guided relaxation |

**Character:** Focused, single-theme. No peak pose. Choose one intention (e.g., "opening the hips" or "energizing the spine"). Move efficiently. Avoid lengthy transitions. Use Sun Salutation A only.

---

### Key Structural Principles

- **Arc of energy:** Classes build energy through the middle and release it at the end. Never peak at the end.
- **Counter-poses:** Every deep backbend needs a gentle forward fold. Every deep twist needs a neutral spine. Every inversion needs grounding.
- **Rule of 3:** Three rounds of Sun Salutations is the minimum for generating body heat in a standing practice.
- **Left before right:** Always cue the left side first (moves students from right-brain/receptive state).
- **Savasana is non-negotiable:** At minimum 1 minute per 10 minutes of practice.

## SECTION 2: SUN SALUTATIONS (SURYA NAMASKAR)

### Sun Salutation A (Surya Namaskar A)

**Sanskrit:** Surya Namaskar A | **German:** Sonnengruß A

**Purpose:** Builds heat, establishes breath-movement link, warms spine and hamstrings. Foundation of all Vinyasa practice. Use in early class (rounds 1–3 = warming phase).

**Breath pattern:** Each movement = one breath. Move with the inhale or the exhale — never hold breath.

---

**Full Sequence — Pose by Pose:**

| # | Sanskrit Name | German Name | Breath | Instruction Cue |
|---|---------------|-------------|--------|-----------------|
| 1 | Samasthiti / Tadasana | Berghaltung / Gleichgewichtshaltung | — | Stand at the top of your mat, feet together or hip-width. Root through all four corners of your feet. Stack ears over shoulders over hips. Hands at heart center. |
| 2 | Urdhva Hastasana | Aufwärtssalutation | Inhale | Sweep the arms out wide and overhead. Reach fingertips to the ceiling. Slight backbend, lifting the chest. Draw the lower ribs in. |
| 3 | Uttanasana | Vorwärtsbeuge (stehend) | Exhale | Swan dive forward, folding from the hip creases. Soften the knees if needed. Let the crown of the head release toward the floor. |
| 4 | Ardha Uttanasana | Halbe Vorwärtsbeuge | Inhale | Lengthen the spine forward. Fingertips to shins or mat. Lift the heart, create a flat-back position. Neck long, gaze slightly ahead. |
| 5 | Plank Pose (Phalakasana) | Planke | Exhale | Step or float back to a high plank. Shoulders over wrists, body in one diagonal line. Press the floor away. Engage the belly. |
| 6 | Chaturanga Dandasana | Vier-Glieder-Stab | Exhale (continue) | Lower to low plank — elbows at 90°, hugging in to the ribs. Elbows track back (not splayed). Shoulders at or above elbow height. |
| 7 | Urdhva Mukha Svanasana | Aufwärts schauender Hund | Inhale | Press through the tops of the feet, lifting chest and thighs off the mat. Straighten the arms. Roll shoulder blades together and down. Gaze forward or slightly up. |
| 8 | Adho Mukha Svanasana | Abwärts schauender Hund | Exhale | Tuck toes, lift hips high. Press hands into the mat, extend the spine. Heels reach toward the floor. Hold 5 breaths. |
| 9 | Ardha Uttanasana | Halbe Vorwärtsbeuge | Inhale | Walk or float feet to hands. Lengthen the spine, flat back, fingertips to shins. |
| 10 | Uttanasana | Vorwärtsbeuge | Exhale | Release down, crown heavy, knees soft. |
| 11 | Urdhva Hastasana | Aufwärtssalutation | Inhale | Sweep arms wide and overhead, rising to stand. Slight backbend. |
| 12 | Samasthiti / Tadasana | Berghaltung | Exhale | Return to Mountain Pose. Hands to heart. |

**Repetitions:**
- Beginners: 3–5 rounds at a slow pace
- Intermediate: 5–8 rounds, including 2–3 held in Downward Dog for 5 breaths
- Advanced: 8–10 rounds with float-throughs, jumping

**Modifications for Beginners:**
- Replace Chaturanga with knees-chest-chin (Ashtanga Namaskara)
- Step back instead of floating/jumping
- Bend knees deeply in all forward folds
- Use blocks under hands in Uttanasana
- Baby Cobra instead of full Upward Dog

### Sun Salutation B (Surya Namaskar B)

**Sanskrit:** Surya Namaskar B | **German:** Sonnengruß B

**Purpose:** Deeper heat, adds Utkatasana (Chair) and Warrior I. Longer sequence. Used after Surya A has warmed the body. Typically taught after 3+ rounds of A.

---

| # | Sanskrit Name | German Name | Breath | Instruction Cue |

| 2 | Utkatasana | Stuhlhaltung / Krafthaltung | Inhale | Bend the knees deeply, sink the hips as if sitting in a chair. Sweep arms overhead. Chest lifts. Knees track over second toes. |
| 3 | Uttanasana | Vorwärtsbeuge | Exhale | Fold forward, bringing hands to the mat. |
| 4 | Ardha Uttanasana | Halbe Vorwärtsbeuge | Inhale | Flat back, lengthen spine. |
| 5 | Chaturanga Dandasana | Vier-Glieder-Stab | Exhale | Step or float back, lower to low plank. |
| 6 | Urdhva Mukha Svanasana | Aufwärts schauender Hund | Inhale | Press up into Upward Dog. |
| 7 | Adho Mukha Svanasana | Abwärts schauender Hund | Exhale | Lift to Down Dog. |
| 8 | Virabhadrasana I (right) | Krieger I (rechts) | Inhale | Step right foot forward between hands. Back foot pivots to 45°. Rise up, arms overhead. Front knee over ankle. Hips square forward. |
| 9 | Chaturanga Dandasana | Vier-Glieder-Stab | Exhale | Plant hands, step back through Plank, lower to Chaturanga. |
| 10 | Urdhva Mukha Svanasana | Aufwärts schauender Hund | Inhale | Upward Dog. |
| 11 | Adho Mukha Svanasana | Abwärts schauender Hund | Exhale | Down Dog. |

| 13 | Chaturanga Dandasana | Vier-Glieder-Stab | Exhale | Lower through Chaturanga. |
| 14 | Urdhva Mukha Svanasana | Aufwärts schauender Hund | Inhale | Upward Dog. |
| 15 | Adho Mukha Svanasana | Abwärts schauender Hund | Exhale | Down Dog. Hold 5 breaths. |
| 16 | Ardha Uttanasana | Halbe Vorwärtsbeuge | Inhale | Walk or float to the top of the mat, flat back. |
| 17 | Uttanasana | Vorwärtsbeuge | Exhale | Fold in. |
| 18 | Utkatasana | Stuhlhaltung | Inhale | Bend knees, lift arms — Chair Pose again. |
| 19 | Samasthiti | Berghaltung | Exhale | Straighten legs, lower arms, return to Mountain. |

**When to Use A vs B:**
- **A alone:** Beginners, 30-min classes, warm-up only, gentle/restorative classes
- **A then B:** All standard Vinyasa classes once body is warm (after 3–5 rounds of A)
- **B alone (advanced):** With students who know the sequence well, as second-round intensifier

## SECTION 3: KEY STANDING POSES (15+)

### 1. Tadasana — Mountain Pose
**German:** Berghaltung  
**Hold:** 5–10 breaths or as a reset between poses

**Setup Cues:**
Stand with feet together (or hip-width for beginners). Spread the toes wide. Lift the inner arches. Engage the thighs gently upward. Tuck the tailbone slightly. Lengthen the spine. Roll the shoulders back and down. Palms face forward. Crown of the head lifts.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Weight distributed evenly across all four corners of each foot
- Knees soft (not locked)
- Neutral pelvis — neither tucked nor overarched
- Ears stacked over shoulders, shoulders over hips, hips over ankles

**Common Mistakes:**
- Locking the knees (hyperextension)
- Collapsing into one hip
- Jutting the chin forward
- Ribs flaring out

**Modifications:**
- Stand with feet hip-width for better balance
- Use a wall for proprioceptive feedback

**Benefits:** Improves posture, builds body awareness, activates postural muscles, grounds nervous system.

---

### 2. Virabhadrasana I — Warrior I
**German:** Krieger I  
**Hold:** 5–8 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
From Downward Dog, step the right foot forward between the hands. Back foot pivots to a 45° angle. The back heel presses into the earth. Rise up, lifting arms overhead, palms facing or touching. Bend the front knee to 90°, knee directly over the ankle. Square the hips toward the front of the mat.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Front knee tracks over the second toe, not collapsing inward
- Back leg active — thigh engaged, not collapsed
- Hips as square as possible (back hip rotates forward)
- Tailbone drops down as the lower back lengthens
- Shoulders release away from ears even as arms lift

**Common Mistakes:**
- Front knee diving inward
- Short stance (not enough room to sink the hip)
- Overarching the lower back
- Collapsing into the front hip
- Arms lifting with tension in the neck

**Modifications:**
- Shorten the stance for tight hips
- Keep hands on hips for shoulder/neck issues
- Crescent Lunge (back heel lifted) for tight Achilles/ankles

**Benefits:** Strengthens legs, glutes, core; opens hip flexors; develops focus and stamina.

---

### 3. Virabhadrasana II — Warrior II
**German:** Krieger II  
**Hold:** 5–8 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
From a wide stance, turn the right foot out 90° and the left foot in slightly. Bend the right knee to 90°. Extend arms out to the sides, parallel to the floor. Gaze over the right fingertips. Keep the torso upright — do not lean forward or back.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Front knee stacks over the ankle, not beyond the toes
- Hips open to the long edge of the mat (not forward)
- Both arms extend actively — feel the pull in both directions
- Back leg straight and strong
- Tailbone drops, lower belly engages

**Common Mistakes:**
- Torso leaning forward (collapsing into the front leg)
- Front knee falling inward
- Rear foot heel lifting
- Shoulders creeping up toward ears

**Modifications:**
- Reduce depth of front-knee bend
- Rest hands on thighs
- Use a chair for support

**Benefits:** Builds leg endurance, hip stability, lateral body opening, focus (drishti — gaze point).

---

### 4. Virabhadrasana III — Warrior III
**German:** Krieger III  
**Hold:** 5–8 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
From Warrior I, shift weight onto the front foot. Hinge forward at the hip as the back leg floats up. Bring the body parallel to the floor — arms forward, torso, and back leg all in one horizontal line. Flex the back foot, toes pointing down.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Standing hip directly over the standing heel
- Both hips level (lifted hip tends to open — draw it down)
- Core deeply engaged — this is the stabilizer
- Spine long, not rounded
- Gaze soft, to the floor ahead

**Common Mistakes:**
- Lifted hip rotating open
- Rounding the spine
- Looking up (destabilizes balance)
- Letting the back foot go limp (flex it!)

**Modifications:**
- Hands on blocks or a wall
- "T-stand" with hands on hips
- Use a chair for the lifted leg

**Benefits:** Full-body strength integration, proprioception, hip stability, hamstring and glute activation.

---

### 5. Trikonasana — Triangle Pose
**German:** Dreieckshaltung  
**Hold:** 5–8 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
Wide stance, right foot out 90°, left foot slightly in. Straighten both legs. Extend the torso out over the right leg (hinging at the hip, not the waist), then lower the right hand to the shin, a block, or the floor. Extend the left arm straight up. Open the chest toward the ceiling.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Hips stack (left hip stays over the right, don't let it collapse back)
- Both legs straight and active — kneecaps lifted
- Top arm extends directly out of the shoulder
- Long spine — don't crunch the lower side
- Neck is a continuation of the spine — gaze up, or down if neck is strained

**Common Mistakes:**
- Collapsing the front knee (must stay straight)
- Bending at the waist instead of the hip hinge
- Twisting the torso forward rather than opening it up
- Putting too much weight into the bottom hand (it's a guide, not a crutch)

**Modifications:**
- Block under the lower hand
- Hand on shin or thigh
- Stand near a wall for support

**Benefits:** Lateral body stretch, hamstring flexibility, hip stability, spinal mobility, IT band release.

---

### 6. Parsvakonasana — Extended Side Angle
**German:** Seitliche Winkelhaltung  
**Hold:** 5–8 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
From Warrior II, lower the right forearm to the right thigh (or the hand to a block or the floor outside the right foot). Extend the left arm over the ear, palm down. Create one long diagonal line from the outer left foot through the left fingertips. Look up under the left arm.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Front knee stays at 90° — don't let it collapse
- Both sides of the waist stay long (don't crunch the lower side)
- Top arm actively reaches — engage the oblique
- Back foot pressed firmly into the earth
- Lower shoulder doesn't collapse

**Common Mistakes:**
- Forearm on knee dumps all weight in (use the thigh as a shelf, not a crutch)
- Top hip rolling backward
- Chest facing down rather than opening upward
- Short stance making it impossible to create the diagonal line

**Modifications:**
- Forearm on thigh
- Block under lower hand
- Gaze down if neck is strained

**Benefits:** Deep lateral stretch, strengthens legs and core, opens chest and shoulders, improves hip mobility.

---

### 7. Parsvottanasana — Pyramid Pose
**German:** Pyramidenhaltung  
**Hold:** 5–8 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
Step the right foot forward, left foot back at 45–60°. Both legs straight. Square the hips forward. Fold over the front leg, reaching the crown of the head toward the shin. Hands on blocks, prayer behind the back, or on the floor.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Hip crease over the front foot (don't reach with the chest — hinge the hip)
- Both hips square forward — back hip must internally rotate
- Front knee soft if hamstrings are tight
- Spine long before releasing into the fold

**Common Mistakes:**
- Twisting the hips open
- Crunching into the fold rather than hinging
- Locking the front knee
- Rear foot turning out too far

**Modifications:**
- Blocks under both hands
- Bend the front knee significantly
- Short stance for tight hamstrings

**Benefits:** Intense hamstring stretch, hip flexor release, challenges balance and hip alignment.

---

### 8. Prasarita Padottanasana — Wide-Legged Forward Fold
**German:** Weitbeinige Vorwärtsbeuge  
**Hold:** 8–10 breaths

**Setup Cues:**
Step feet wide (roughly one leg-length apart), feet parallel or slightly pigeon-toed. Hands on hips, inhale to lengthen. Exhale, fold forward from the hip creases. Bring hands to the mat (or blocks). Crown of the head may reach toward the floor.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Feet parallel — check that outer edges are parallel
- Weight over balls of feet, not heels
- Inner thighs rotating back (engaging the adductors)
- Spine long — don't force the head to the floor by rounding

**Modifications:**
- Blocks under the hands
- Hands clasped behind the back (Variation C — shoulder opener)
- Bend the knees if hamstrings are tight

**Benefits:** Inner thigh and hamstring stretch, spinal decompression, calming inversion.

---

### 9. Utkatasana — Chair Pose
**German:** Stuhlhaltung / Krafthaltung  
**Hold:** 5–10 breaths

**Setup Cues:**
Feet together or hip-width. Bend the knees deeply as if sitting into an invisible chair. Sit the hips back and down. Lift the arms overhead (or keep them parallel). Chest lifts, gaze forward or slightly up. Keep the spine long — the tendency is to round.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Weight back in the heels — can you wiggle your toes?
- Knees track over second toes — don't let them collapse in
- Tailbone drops (don't overarch the lumbar)
- Lower belly lightly engages
- Shoulders away from ears

**Common Mistakes:**
- Heels lifting
- Knees past the toes
- Overarching the lower back
- Collapsing the chest

**Benefits:** Strengthens quads, glutes, core; builds heat and stamina; develops mental fortitude.

---

### 10. Uttanasana — Standing Forward Fold
**German:** Stehende Vorwärtsbeuge  
**Hold:** 5–10 breaths

**Setup Cues:**
Feet hip-width or together. Fold forward from the hip creases, not the waist. Let the head hang heavy. Hands to shins, blocks, or the floor. Micro-bend the knees if needed.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Fold at the hip joint, not the lumbar spine
- Weight over the balls of the feet
- Upper body relaxed — shake the head slowly yes/no
- Each exhale invites a little more release

**Benefits:** Hamstring and calf stretch, spinal decompression, calming for the nervous system.

---

### 11. Malasana — Garland Pose / Yogi Squat
**German:** Girlanden-Haltung / Yoga-Hocke  
**Hold:** 8–10 breaths

**Setup Cues:**
Step feet mat-width apart, toes turned out about 45°. Lower into a deep squat. Bring the elbows inside the knees and press the palms together at the heart. Use the elbows to gently encourage the knees wide. Keep the chest lifted.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Heels on the floor (or on a folded blanket if heels lift)
- Sit bones drop toward the floor
- Torso long, not collapsed

**Modifications:**
- Blanket under heels
- Sit on a block

**Benefits:** Deep hip and groin opener, ankle mobility, lower back release.

---

### 12. Viparita Virabhadrasana — Reverse Warrior
**German:** Umgekehrter Krieger  
**Hold:** 3–5 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
From Warrior II, flip the front palm to face the sky. Slide the back hand down the back leg. Lift the front arm up and back, arching gently into a lateral backbend. Keep the front knee bent.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Front knee stays at 90° — don't straighten it
- Side body opens — feel the stretch along the front arm's side
- Back hand is light on the leg — not weight-bearing

**Benefits:** Lateral body opening, intercostal stretch, counterbalances Warrior II.

---

### 13. Ardha Chandrasana — Half Moon Pose
**German:** Halbmondhaltung  
**Hold:** 5–8 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
From Triangle, bend the front knee slightly, place a hand on a block (or the floor) about 12 inches ahead and to the right. Shift weight onto the front foot and lift the back leg parallel to the floor. Stack the hips, open the chest, extend the top arm skyward.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Standing leg not locked — micro-bend
- Both hips stacked (one over the other in full expression)
- Lifted foot active — flex it or extend through the heel
- Core engaged — this is essential for balance

**Common Mistakes:**
- Top hip collapsing back (the most common error)
- Using the hand for weight-bearing (it's for balance only)
- Gaze up when neck isn't ready (look down first)

**Modifications:**
- Block under bottom hand
- Use wall for back foot support
- Keep gaze down

**Benefits:** Hip abductor strength, balance, whole-body integration.

---

### 14. Garudasana — Eagle Pose
**German:** Adlerhaltung  
**Hold:** 5–8 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
From Mountain, bend the knees slightly. Cross the right thigh over the left. Try to hook the right foot behind the left calf. Cross the right arm under the left, then bring the palms together (or backs of hands together). Lift the elbows and lower the weight into the standing leg.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Sitting low into the standing leg deepens the pose
- Elbows at shoulder height
- Gaze soft — find one still point (drishti)
- Shoulders draw away from ears even as elbows lift

**Modifications:**
- Crossed leg: toes on the floor instead of wrapped behind calf
- Arms: simple cross at the elbows if hands can't touch

**Benefits:** Hip and IT band release, shoulder stretch, deep balance challenge.

---

### 15. Vrksasana — Tree Pose
**German:** Baumhaltung  
**Hold:** 8–10 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
Stand on the left foot. Place the right foot on the inner left calf or inner thigh (never the knee). Bring the hands to heart center or extend them overhead. Find a drishti — a still, soft gaze point.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Standing leg: micro-bend, foot rooted
- Placed foot: actively presses into the standing leg (they press into each other)
- Hips level — the lifted-leg hip tends to hike
- Core lightly engaged

**Modifications:**
- Toes on the floor (kickstand Tree)
- Use a wall for support

**Benefits:** Balance, proprioception, hip stability, mental focus.

---

### 16. Natarajasana — Dancer's Pose / Lord of the Dance
**German:** Tänzerhaltung / Natarajasana  
**Hold:** 5–8 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
Stand on the left foot. Bend the right knee, reaching the right hand back to hold the inner right ankle. Extend the left arm forward. Kick the right foot into the hand as you hinge forward slightly. Lift and arch. Find the balance between the kick and the reach forward.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Hips level (don't open the back hip)
- Kicking foot creates the backbend — don't just pull
- Gazing forward helps balance
- Core engaged

**Modifications:**
- Use a wall or strap
- Gentle version: smaller kick, less forward hinge

**Benefits:** Backbend, hip flexor stretch, balance, full-body coordination.

---

### 17. Parivrtta Trikonasana — Revolved Triangle
**German:** Gedrehte Dreieckshaltung  
**Hold:** 5–8 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
From Pyramid Pose stance (right foot forward), place the left hand to the outside of the right foot (on a block). Extend the right arm up. Twist the torso to the right, opening the chest toward the ceiling.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Hips stay as square as possible (most challenging part)
- Twist initiates from the thoracic spine, not just the shoulder
- Both legs straight and active
- Lower hand is a guide — don't dump weight

**Modifications:**
- Block under lower hand (mandatory for most)
- Bend the front knee

**Benefits:** Deep spinal rotation, hamstring stretch, hip realignment, digestive stimulation.

### 4. Utthita Hasta Padangusthasana — Extended Hand-to-Big-Toe Pose
**German:** Stehende Hand-zu-Zeh-Haltung  
**Hold:** 5–8 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
Stand on the left foot. Bend the right knee to the chest, take hold of the big toe with the right index and middle fingers (or use a strap). Begin to extend the right leg forward. If stable, open the leg to the right side.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Standing leg straight and strong
- Hip of lifted leg not hiking up
- Spine tall — don't hunch over the lifted leg
- Free hand on hip for stability

**Modifications:**
- Strap around the foot
- Keep the knee bent
- Hand on the knee instead of the foot

**Benefits:** Hamstring flexibility, hip stability, intense balance challenge.

### 6. Bakasana — Crow Pose
**German:** Krähenhaltung  
**Hold:** 5–10 breaths

**Setup Cues:**
From a squat, place hands shoulder-width on the floor. Bend the elbows slightly. Lean forward and rest the knees on the backs of the upper arms (as high as possible). Shift weight forward until the feet lift off. Gaze forward, not down.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Knees high on the arms (near armpits = more stable)
- Core very engaged — lift the belly
- Elbows at 90° — not straight
- Gaze forward — looking down = tipping forward

**Modifications:**
- Blocks under the feet for height
- Blanket in front for psychological safety
- Try lifting one foot at a time first

**Benefits:** Core and wrist strength, arm balance, courage, proprioception.

### 8. Parivrtta Ardha Chandrasana — Revolved Half Moon
**German:** Gedrehter Halbmond  
**Hold:** 3–5 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
From Warrior III, bring the bottom hand to a block directly under the shoulder. Rotate the top arm down toward the floor (twist), then extend it up. Both arms form a vertical line. Gaze can be down, forward, or up.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Hips as level as possible (the hardest part)
- Twist comes from the thoracic spine
- Standing leg stable — micro-bend

**Benefits:** Peak pose integrating balance, core strength, rotation, and hip stability.

## SECTION 5: HIP OPENING POSES (8+)

### 1. Anjaneyasana — Low Lunge
**German:** Niedriger Ausfallschritt / Mondsichelpose  
**Hold:** 8–10 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
From Down Dog, step the right foot forward between the hands. Lower the left knee to the mat (pad it if needed). Square the hips. Sweep the arms up and overhead. Lift the chest. Allow the left hip flexor to release.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Front knee over the ankle, not beyond
- Back knee under the hip (or slightly behind for deeper hip flexor stretch)
- Tailbone drops down, lower belly engages — don't overarch
- Heart lifts, not just the lower back

**Common Mistakes:**
- Front knee collapsing inward
- Overarching the lumbar (tuck the tailbone slightly)
- Hips not square (often one hip is hiked)

**Modifications:**
- Blanket under the back knee
- Hands on blocks instead of reaching up
- Crescent Lunge (back knee off the ground)

**Benefits:** Hip flexor and psoas stretch (essential for modern humans who sit), quadriceps stretch, chest opener.

---

### 2. Eka Pada Rajakapotasana — Pigeon Pose (prep)
**German:** Taubenhaltung  
**Hold:** 1–3 minutes per side

**Setup Cues:**
From Down Dog, bring the right knee forward and place it behind the right wrist. Extend the right shin diagonally across the mat. Extend the left leg straight back. Square the hips toward the front of the mat. Walk the hands forward and fold over the right shin. Rest on the forearms or lay fully forward.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Right shin as horizontal as the hip flexibility allows (beginners: more diagonal)
- Both hip points level toward the mat
- Back leg extending straight back (not splaying to the side)
- Right foot flexed protects the knee

**Common Mistakes:**
- Collapsing all weight into the bent-leg hip (prop it with a blanket if needed)
- Forcing the shin horizontal before the hips allow it (knee strain risk)
- Back foot turning in or out

**Modifications:**
- Blanket or block under the right hip
- Sleeping Swan on a blanket
- Supine Figure-4 (Eye of the Needle) — same benefit, safer for knee

**Benefits:** Deep external hip rotator stretch (piriformis, glute medius), psoas release, emotional release (hips store tension).

---

### 3. Gomukhasana — Cow Face Pose
**German:** Kuhgesichtshaltung  
**Hold:** 1–2 minutes per side

**Setup Cues:**
Sit with legs stacked — right knee over left. (Sit on a block if the lower hip lifts.) Both feet flex, outside of each foot on the floor. Walk the hips under you. For arms: right arm up and bend the elbow, left arm behind the back and reach up — clasp hands (or use a strap).

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Sit bones even on the floor (or block)
- Spine tall before adding arm variation
- Knees stacked vertically

**Modifications:**
- Sit on a block
- Use a strap for arms
- Skip the arm position and focus on the legs

**Benefits:** Deep external hip rotator stretch, shoulder mobility (arm variation), IT band release.

---

### 4. Baddha Konasana — Bound Angle / Butterfly Pose
**German:** Gebundener Winkelsitz / Schmetterlingssitz  
**Hold:** 1–3 minutes

**Setup Cues:**
Sit on the floor (or a blanket). Bring the soles of the feet together, letting the knees fall out to the sides. Hold the feet or ankles. Inhale to lengthen the spine; exhale to hinge forward from the hip creases (not by rounding the back).

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Spine long — don't round aggressively
- Heels as close to the pelvis as is comfortable
- Let the knees fall naturally — don't force them down

**Modifications:**
- Sit on a blanket or blocks
- Place blocks under the knees for support

**Benefits:** Inner thigh and groin stretch, hip joint opening, calming.

---

### 5. Supta Kapotasana — Reclined Pigeon / Eye of the Needle
**German:** Liegende Taubenhaltung / Fadennadelhaltung  
**Hold:** 1–3 minutes per side

**Setup Cues:**
Lie on your back. Cross the right ankle over the left thigh. Flex the right foot. For deeper stretch, lift the left foot off the floor, threading the right hand through the gap and interlacing hands behind the left thigh. Draw the left thigh toward the chest.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Right foot flexed protects the knee
- Keep the lower back on the mat
- Both shoulders on the floor

**Benefits:** Safer alternative to Pigeon; same piriformis and glute stretch. Excellent for all levels and for lower back relief.

---

### 6. Lizard Pose — Utthan Pristhasana
**German:** Eidechsenhaltung  
**Hold:** 1–2 minutes per side

**Setup Cues:**
From Low Lunge (right foot forward), walk the right foot to the outside of the right hand. Lower the left knee. For deeper variation, come onto the forearms (use blocks under the forearms). Keep the back leg active.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Right knee tracks over the right ankle
- Hips sinking toward the floor, not hiking up
- Back leg lengthening — don't dump into it

**Benefits:** Deep hip flexor and groin stretch, prepares for splits.

---

### 7. Agnistambhasana — Fire Log Pose / Double Pigeon
**German:** Feuerstapelhaltung  
**Hold:** 1–2 minutes per side

**Setup Cues:**
Sit with the right shin parallel to the front edge of the mat, left shin stacked on top with left ankle over right knee, left knee over right ankle. Flex both feet. Sit tall, then fold forward from the hips.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Both feet flexed throughout
- Shins horizontal (parallel to the mat — this is the ideal)
- Don't collapse the upper shin (prop if needed)

**Modifications:**
- One or both blankets under the stacked shins
- Use Baddha Konasana instead if too intense

**Benefits:** One of the deepest external hip rotator stretches available. Excellent for runners, cyclists, and anyone with tight glutes.

---

### 8. Virasana / Supta Virasana — Hero's Pose / Reclined Hero
**German:** Heldensitz / Liegender Heldensitz  
**Hold:** 1–5 minutes

**Setup Cues (Virasana):**
Kneel with knees together, feet slightly wider than hip-width. Sit down between the heels (or on a block between the feet). Stack the spine. Hands on thighs.

**Supta Virasana:** Lean back, coming onto the forearms or lying fully back. Arms extend overhead or rest on the belly.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Knees comfortable — prop the hips to reduce knee pressure
- Feet alongside the hips (not under them)
- Lower back long in full recline

**Modifications:**
- Block or bolster under the sitting bones
- Blankets under the knees
- Stay upright (don't recline) if knees are sensitive

**Benefits:** Quadriceps and hip flexor stretch, ankle mobility, knee joint opening (when done safely).

## SECTION 6: BACKBENDS (6+)

### 1. Bhujangasana — Cobra Pose
**German:** Kobrähaltung  
**Hold:** 3–5 breaths

**Setup Cues:**
Lie on your belly. Place hands under the shoulders, elbows close to the ribs. Press the tops of the feet and the pubic bone into the mat. On an inhale, press through the hands and lift the chest. Elbows may stay slightly bent. Roll the shoulders back and down.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Elbows bent — this is Cobra, not Upward Dog
- Pubic bone stays on the mat
- Shoulders away from ears
- Chin and gaze slightly up, but neck long

**Common Mistakes:**
- Straightening the arms (that's Upward Dog)
- Crunching the neck back
- Bowing the elbows out

**Modifications:**
- "Baby Cobra" — very low lift, elbows bent deeply, hands may hover

**Benefits:** Spinal extension, chest and lung opening, strengthens back muscles.

---

### 2. Urdhva Mukha Svanasana — Upward Facing Dog
**German:** Aufwärts schauender Hund  
**Hold:** 1 breath (in Sun Salutation) or 3–5 breaths

**Setup Cues:**
From the floor, press through the hands to straighten the arms. Lift the thighs off the mat. Only tops of feet and hands touch the ground. Roll shoulder blades together and down. Chest expands. Gaze forward or slightly up.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Thighs completely off the floor (unlike Cobra)
- Shoulders directly over the wrists
- Core lightly engaged — lower back shouldn't crunch
- Tops of feet pressing down equally

**Common Mistakes:**
- Thighs on the mat (then it's Cobra)
- Shoulders shrugging up
- Head craning back excessively

**Benefits:** Deeper spinal extension than Cobra, chest opener, strengthens upper back and arms.

---

### 3. Ustrasana — Camel Pose
**German:** Kamelhaltung  
**Hold:** 5–8 breaths

**Setup Cues:**
Kneel with knees hip-width. Tuck the toes under (or point feet flat). Place hands on the lower back, fingers pointing down. Inhale to lift the chest. Begin to lean back, eventually reaching hands to the heels. Let the head release (or keep it neutral if that's more comfortable).

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Thighs vertical — don't let them drift back as you lean
- Hips press forward as the chest opens back
- Lower ribs in — don't flare them
- Hands on heels (not the ankles — can strain the wrist)

**Common Mistakes:**
- Thighs falling back (losing the upright-thigh line)
- Crunching into the lower back (it's a chest opener, not a lumbar crunch)
- Head dropping back with neck tension

**Modifications:**
- Hands on blocks beside the feet (raised heels)
- Stay with hands on lower back
- Tuck toes for a higher heel position

**Benefits:** Thoracic extension, chest and shoulder opening, hip flexor stretch, counteracts forward-head posture.

---

### 4. Urdhva Dhanurasana — Wheel Pose / Full Backbend
**German:** Radhaltung / Vollständige Rückbeuge  
**Hold:** 3–5 breaths, 1–3 rounds

**Setup Cues:**
Lie on your back. Bend the knees, feet hip-width, heels close to the sitting bones. Place hands by the ears, fingers pointing toward the shoulders. Press through hands and feet equally, lift the hips and chest, extending the arms. The body forms an arch. 

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Feet parallel — don't turn them out
- Knees tracking over feet — not splaying or squeezing
- Press the floor away — don't collapse into the wrists
- Thoracic spine extends (the goal — not lumbar crunch)

**Common Mistakes:**
- Feet turning out (externalizes the femur, blocks the opening)
- Elbows splaying wide (should point up and back)
- Rounding the upper back instead of opening the thoracic
- Rushing — this is a peak pose requiring preparation

**Preparation Poses Required:** Bridge, Camel, Upward Dog, shoulder and wrist warm-up

**Modifications:**
- Bridge Pose (Setu Bandha Sarvangasana)
- Supported Bridge with a block under the sacrum
- Wheel with feet on blocks

**Benefits:** Full spinal extension, deep chest and shoulder opening, energizing, counteracts sitting posture.

---

### 5. Setu Bandha Sarvangasana — Bridge Pose
**German:** Brückenhaltung  
**Hold:** 5–10 breaths, or hold 1–3 minutes with block support

**Setup Cues:**
Lie on your back. Bend the knees, feet hip-width and flat on the floor, heels close to the sitting bones. Inhale and press through the feet to lift the hips. Interlace the hands under the back and roll the shoulders under. Lift the chest toward the chin.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Feet parallel, hip-width
- Knees tracking over the feet
- Thighs parallel to the floor at full height
- Chin away from the chest (create space at the throat)

**Modifications:**
- Block under the sacrum (Supported Bridge — restorative)
- Keep arms alongside the body rather than interlacing

**Benefits:** Spinal extension, glute and hamstring strengthening, chest opener, accessible backbend for all levels.

---

### 6. Dhanurasana — Bow Pose
**German:** Bogenhaltung  
**Hold:** 3–5 breaths, 1–3 rounds

**Setup Cues:**
Lie on the belly. Bend the knees and reach back to hold the outer ankles. On an inhale, kick the feet into the hands, lifting the chest and thighs off the mat. The body resembles a drawn bow. Gaze forward.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Kick creates the lift — both ends of the bow must work
- Knees hip-width (not splaying wider)
- Pubic bone stays as the anchor point
- Breathe — the belly on the mat makes breathing feel restricted; short breaths are normal

**Modifications:**
- Use a strap around the ankles
- Only one leg at a time (Half Bow)

**Benefits:** Full anterior body stretch, spinal extension, glute and hamstring strengthening.

## SECTION 7: FORWARD FOLDS (6+)

### 1. Paschimottanasana — Seated Forward Fold
**German:** Sitzende Vorwärtsbeuge / Westliche Dehnung  
**Hold:** 1–3 minutes

**Setup Cues:**
Sit with legs extended. Flex the feet. Inhale to lengthen the spine. Exhale to hinge forward from the hip creases, walking the hands along the legs. Aim for length over depth — a long spine folding is more valuable than a rounded crunch.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Hip crease fold (not waist-crunch)
- Spine long
- Flex the feet actively throughout
- Micro-bend the knees if hamstrings are very tight

**Modifications:**
- Strap around the feet
- Blanket under the knees
- Bend the knees significantly

**Benefits:** Hamstring and calf stretch, spinal decompression, calming for the nervous system.

### 2. Janu Sirsasana — Head-to-Knee Forward Fold
**German:** Kopf-zum-Knie-Haltung  
**Hold:** 1–2 minutes per side

**Setup Cues:**
Sit with legs extended. Bend the right knee and place the right foot against the inner left thigh. Square the torso toward the extended left leg. Inhale to lengthen; exhale to fold forward over the left leg.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Torso faces the extended leg, not the center
- Right knee on the floor or supported by a blanket
- Both sitting bones on the floor

**Modifications:**
- Blanket under the bent knee
- Strap around the extended foot

**Benefits:** Asymmetrical hamstring stretch, mild twist, kidney and liver massage.

### 3. Balasana — Child's Pose
**German:** Kindshaltung  
**Hold:** As long as needed — typically 5–10 breaths or up to 2 minutes

**Setup Cues:**
From kneeling, bring the big toes together and knees wide (or knees together for Extended Child's). Sit back toward the heels. Extend the arms forward, forehead to the mat. Breathe into the back body.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Hips as close to heels as possible (use blanket between hips and calves if gap is large)
- Forehead on the mat (or on a block)
- Arms actively extended or relaxed beside the body

**Modifications:**
- Blanket between the hips and heels
- Bolster under the chest for supported version
- Knees together if wide-knee uncomfortable

**Benefits:** Rest pose, hip and lower back release, calming, spinal decompression.

## SECTION 8: SEATED POSES & TWISTS (6+)

### 1. Ardha Matsyendrasana — Half Lord of the Fishes / Seated Twist
**German:** Halbfischer-Drehsitz  
**Hold:** 8–10 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
Sit with legs extended. Bend the right knee and step the right foot to the outside of the left thigh. Left leg can be straight or bent (heel to right hip). Place the left elbow outside the right knee, right hand behind you. On each inhale, grow taller; on each exhale, deepen the twist.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Both sitting bones on the floor
- Spine tall before twisting — the lift is what creates the twist
- Twist begins at the base of the spine and spirals upward
- Chin can follow the twist (look over the right shoulder)

**Common Mistakes:**
- Collapsing the spine (rounding) — the twist will feel flat
- Forcing the elbow against the knee to crank the twist
- One sitting bone lifting

**Modifications:**
- Keep both legs extended (Simple Twist)
- Sit on a blanket

**Benefits:** Spinal rotation, digestion stimulation, chest opener, hip release.

**German:** Gedrehter einfacher Sitz  
**Hold:** 5–8 breaths per side

**Setup Cues:**
Sit cross-legged. Inhale to lengthen the spine. Place the right hand on the left knee and the left hand behind you. Exhale to rotate left. With each inhale, grow taller; with each exhale, rotate gently deeper.

**Benefits:** Gentle warm-up twist, spinal mobility, accessible for all levels.

### 3. Sukhasana — Easy Seat / Cross-Legged Seat

**Modifications:**
- Sit on a block or bolster to tilt the pelvis forward
- Use the wall for back support

**Benefits:** Hip and ankle opening, grounding, meditative stillness.

### 4. Dandasana — Staff Pose
**German:** Stabshaltung

**Alignment Key Points:**

- Thighs pressing toward the floor
- This seemingly simple pose activates the core and back strongly

## SECTION 9: RESTORATIVE / COOL-DOWN POSES (5+)

### 1. Savasana — Corpse Pose
**German:** Totenhaltung / Entspannungshaltung  
**Hold:** 5–15 minutes

**Setup Cues:**
Lie flat on the back. Let the feet fall open naturally. Arms slightly away from the sides, palms facing up. Close the eyes. Release all effort. The body is completely still. The teacher may guide a relaxation or remain silent.

**For the Teacher:** This is not sleep — it is conscious release. Cue students to remain awake but completely passive. Use a soft, slow voice. Offer the silence as a gift.

**Props:**
- Bolster under the knees (relieves lower back)
- Blanket over the body (for warmth — the body temperature drops)
- Eye pillow (blocks light, soothes the nervous system)
- Blanket under the head

**Benefits:** Integration of the practice, parasympathetic activation, stress hormone reduction, the most important pose in yoga.

---

### 2. Supta Baddha Konasana — Reclined Butterfly / Bound Angle
**German:** Liegender Schmetterlingssitz  
**Hold:** 3–5 minutes

**Setup Cues:**
Lie on the back. Bring the soles of the feet together and let the knees fall wide. Place hands on the belly or arms wide. Support the knees with blocks or rolled blankets. Close the eyes and breathe.

**Benefits:** Passive hip opener, chest opener, deeply calming, no muscular effort required.

---

### 3. Viparita Karani — Legs Up the Wall
**German:** Beine-an-der-Wand-Haltung  
**Hold:** 5–15 minutes

**Setup Cues:**
Sit sideways against a wall, then swing the legs up. Scoot the hips as close to the wall as comfortable. Arms rest wide, palms up. Close the eyes.

**Modifications:**
- Bolster under the hips for a mild inversion effect
- Legs slightly away from the wall if hamstrings are tight

**Benefits:** Reverses venous blood flow (excellent for tired legs), calms the nervous system, mild inversion without effort. One of the best restorative poses.

---

### 4. Supta Matsyendrasana — Supine Spinal Twist
**German:** Liegender Drehsitz  
**Hold:** 1–3 minutes per side

**Setup Cues:**
Lie on the back. Draw the right knee to the chest. Let it fall across the body to the left (toward the left hip or floor). Extend the right arm wide, palm down. Turn the gaze right. Shoulders stay on the floor.

**Alignment Key Points:**
- Both shoulders on the mat
- Top arm can rest on the thigh (no forcing)

**Benefits:** Gentle spinal rotation, hip and IT band release, relieves lower back tension.

---

### 5. Matsyasana — Supported Fish Pose
**German:** Fischhaltung  
**Hold:** 3–5 minutes

**Setup Cues (Supported/Restorative version):**
Place a bolster or two stacked blocks lengthwise on the mat. Sit in front of them and lie back so the bolster supports the thoracic spine. The head may rest on a block or blanket. Arms spread wide. Legs can be straight or in Baddha Konasana.

**Benefits:** Counter-pose to all forward bends and sitting postures, opens the chest, thoracic extension, deeply restorative.

### 6. Constructive Rest Pose (Semi-Supine)
**German:** Konstruktive Ruheposition  
**Hold:** 3–10 minutes

**Setup Cues:**
Lie on the back with knees bent, feet flat on the floor hip-width apart. Let the lower back soften. Arms alongside the body or hands on the belly. Allow gravity to do the work.

**Benefits:** Releases chronic lower back tension, grounds the nervous system, excellent between active poses and Savasana.

## SECTION 10: PRANAYAMA (BREATHWORK)

### Ujjayi Breath — Ocean Breath
**Sanskrit:** Ujjayi | **German:** Ujjayi-Atem / Meeresatem

**What it is:**
A breath technique where a slight constriction is applied at the back of the throat (glottis), creating an oceanic, whispering sound on both inhale and exhale. It's the breath of Vinyasa yoga.

**How to do it:**
1. Breathe in through the nose. On the exhale, open the mouth and breathe out with a "Haaah" — like fogging a mirror.
2. Close the mouth and make that same constriction with the throat on the exhale through the nose.
3. Apply the same constriction on the inhale.
4. The sound is audible to yourself (not to others in the room unless very close).

**The sound:** Like the ocean, or Darth Vader — soft and continuous.

**When to use in class:**
- Introduce it during the warm-up (Cat/Cow or early Sun Salutations)
- Maintain throughout the standing practice — it regulates pace and internal heat
- Let it go (soften to natural breath) in restorative poses and Savasana
- Cue it when students look scattered or rushing

**Benefits:**
- Generates internal heat (thermoregulation)
- Extends the breath — slows the practice
- Focuses the mind — the sound becomes a moving meditation
- Tones the vagus nerve (parasympathetic activation)
- Regulates the pace of movement

**Teacher Cue:** *"Breathe only through your nose, creating a soft ocean sound at the back of your throat. Let your breath be the soundtrack of your practice."*

---

### Nadi Shodhana — Alternate Nostril Breathing
**Sanskrit:** Nadi Shodhana | **German:** Wechselatmung / Kanal-Reinigung

**What it is:**
A pranayama technique where the breath alternates between left and right nostrils using the right hand. "Nadi" means energy channel; "Shodhana" means purification. Balances the left (Ida — cooling, lunar) and right (Pingala — heating, solar) nadis.

**Technique (Classic):**
1. Sit comfortably with spine tall.
2. Right hand: Vishnu Mudra — fold index and middle fingers to the palm. Thumb controls the right nostril; ring finger controls the left.
3. Close the right nostril with the thumb. Inhale slowly through the left nostril (count of 4).
4. Close both nostrils (kumbhaka). Pause (count of 2 — optional for beginners, skip if uncomfortable).
5. Release the right nostril. Exhale through the right (count of 4 or 8).
6. Inhale through the right (count of 4).
7. Close both. Pause (count of 2).
8. Release the left nostril. Exhale through the left (count of 4 or 8).
9. This is one round. Practice 5–10 rounds.

**Extended ratio (intermediate):** 4:2:8 (inhale:hold:exhale)

**Benefits:**
- Balances the two hemispheres of the brain
- Reduces stress and anxiety
- Prepares the mind for meditation
- Equalizes energy when one side feels dominant
- Lowers blood pressure, heart rate

**When to teach:**
- At the beginning of class (before centering) — balances the nervous system
- Before meditation at the end of class
- Ideal for stressed or anxious students
- Not recommended immediately after intense physical practice (let the heart rate settle first)

**Teacher Cue:** *"If your mind feels scattered, this breath is your reset button. Let the right hand become an anchor as the breath moves like a tide from one side to the other."*

---

### Box Breathing (Square Breathing)
**German:** Viereckige Atmung / Boxatmung

**Technique:**
1. Inhale for a count of 4
2. Hold (full lungs) for a count of 4
3. Exhale for a count of 4
4. Hold (empty lungs) for a count of 4
5. Repeat 4–8 rounds

**Benefits:**
- Reduces cortisol
- Re-regulates the nervous system after stress
- Accessible to everyone — no special mudra needed
- Used by athletes and military for performance and calm

**When to use:** Opening of class, or when students need grounding. Simple and powerful.

---

### 4-7-8 Breath
**German:** 4-7-8-Atemübung

**Technique:**
1. Exhale completely through the mouth
2. Close the mouth and inhale quietly through the nose to a count of 4
3. Hold the breath for a count of 7
4. Exhale completely through the mouth with a whoosh sound to a count of 8
5. This is one cycle. Repeat 3–4 cycles.

**Benefits:**
- Powerful nervous system regulator
- Promotes sleep onset
- Quickly reduces acute anxiety
- The 8-count exhale activates the parasympathetic system

**When to use:** Before Savasana, for evening classes, for students who can't "switch off."

---

### Three-Part Breath (Dirga Pranayama)
**Sanskrit:** Dirga Pranayama | **German:** Dreistufige Atmung

**Technique:**
1. **Belly (low):** Breathe in and let the belly expand first, like filling a balloon from the bottom. Exhale — belly falls.
2. **Ribcage (mid):** Add the ribcage expanding side-to-side and front-to-back.
3. **Chest (upper):** Finally, let the breath fill the upper chest to the collarbones.
4. Exhale in reverse: chest deflates, ribs soften, belly draws in.

Practice slowly, 5–10 rounds.

**Benefits:**
- Teaches diaphragmatic breathing (reverses chest-breathing pattern)
- Maximizes lung capacity
- Deeply calming
- Excellent for beginners — helps them understand breath mechanics

**When to use:** Very beginning of class — lying down or seated. The best entry point into breathwork for new students.

**Teacher Cue:** *"Imagine the breath is water filling a glass — it fills from the bottom first. Let the belly rise, then the ribs, then the chest."*

---

### Transitioning Between Breath Techniques

**Principles:**
1. **Never force a breath technique.** Invite, suggest, observe.
2. **Move from simple to complex:** Three-Part → Ujjayi → Nadi Shodhana → Box → 4-7-8
3. **Match the breath to the phase of class:**
   - Opening: Three-Part or Nadi Shodhana
   - Active practice: Ujjayi
   - Post-peak / cooling: Natural breath, softer Ujjayi
   - Savasana prep: 4-7-8 or Box
4. **Transition language:** *"Let go of the Ujjayi breath now. Allow the breath to return to its natural rhythm..."*
5. **Never introduce a new technique during a challenging pose** — save it for stillness.

## SECTION 11: MEDITATION TECHNIQUES (5 Guided Scripts)

### 1. Body Scan (5–7 minutes)
**German:** Körper-Scan

*Begin in Savasana or a comfortable seat.*

"Close your eyes, and let your body fully surrender to the ground beneath you. Take three deep, natural breaths. Let each exhale be a release — a letting go of everything that came before this moment.

Now bring your awareness to your feet. Notice the soles of your feet. Without changing anything, simply notice — warmth, coolness, tingling, or nothing at all. Whatever is there, is welcome.

Slowly let your awareness rise to the ankles... the calves... the shins. Notice any holding or tension. On your next exhale, invite the calves to soften.

Continue upward to the knees... the thighs — front and back. The thighs carry so much. Thank them for their strength today, and now invite them to rest.

Let your awareness settle into the hips and pelvis. The seat of so much energy and emotion. Simply notice what's there — no need to judge or change.

Move to the belly. Feel how it rises and falls with each breath. Notice the subtle rhythm of life.

Let your awareness expand to the ribcage — the back of the ribs pressing into the earth. The chest opening with each inhale.

Rise to the shoulders, the upper arms, the elbows. The forearms... the wrists... the hands. Feel all ten fingers. Perhaps there's a gentle pulse there.

Now the neck — long and soft. The jaw. Notice if the teeth are touching. Let them part slightly. The tongue — let it rest on the bottom of the mouth. The eyes — soft in their sockets. The forehead — wide and smooth.

The whole body now resting, breathing, and at ease. You are here. You are whole. Take three more complete breaths. And when you're ready, begin to deepen your breath. Let the fingers curl, the toes wiggle. And slowly return."

---

### 2. Breath Awareness (3–4 minutes)
**German:** Atemgewahrsein

*Begin in a comfortable seated position.*

"Gently close your eyes. Let your spine lengthen. Without trying to control anything, simply notice the breath.

Notice the inhale — where do you feel it? Perhaps at the tip of the nose, where the air is slightly cooler than your skin. Perhaps in the rise of the chest. Perhaps in the expansion of the belly.

Now notice the exhale — the release of warmth through the nostrils. The body settling a little more with each breath.

Don't try to change the breath. Simply observe it as you would a river — watching it flow without needing to redirect it.

If a thought arises, notice it as if it were a cloud passing through a blue sky. You are the sky — vast and clear. Thoughts come and go. But you remain.

Come back to the breath. This breath. Right now. The one that is keeping you alive in this very moment.

Feel grateful for this breath. And this one. And this one.

Stay here for a few moments more, watching the tide of your own breathing."

---

### 3. Loving-Kindness Meditation (Metta) (4–5 minutes)
**German:** Liebende-Güte-Meditation

*Seated, eyes closed.*

"Bring your hands to your heart center. Feel the warmth of your own hands against your chest.

Begin by turning this warmth toward yourself. Silently or softly in your mind, repeat these phrases:

*May I be happy.*  
*May I be healthy.*  
*May I be safe.*  
*May I live with ease.*

Let the words land gently. You are deserving of your own love. Let that be true right now.

Now bring to mind someone you love easily — a child, a dear friend, a pet. Feel their presence in your mind's eye.

*May you be happy.*  
*May you be healthy.*  
*May you be safe.*  
*May you live with ease.*

Now bring to mind someone neutral — someone you neither like nor dislike. Perhaps a neighbor, a cashier at the store. They, too, long to be happy.

*May you be happy. May you be healthy. May you be safe. May you live with ease.*

And now — if you're ready — bring to mind someone who is difficult for you. You don't need to like them. Simply acknowledge that they, like you, are trying. They, too, long to be free from suffering.

*May you be happy. May you be healthy. May you be safe. May you live with ease.*

Finally, expand this feeling outward — to this room, this city, this planet. All beings, everywhere, wishing for the same things you wish for.

*May all beings everywhere be happy, healthy, safe, and free.*

Let your hands rest. Breathe. Carry this feeling into the rest of your day."

---

### 4. Visualization Meditation — Forest (3–4 minutes)
**German:** Waldvisualisierung

*Savasana or seated.*

"Allow your eyes to close. Take a few easy breaths. With each exhale, let go of this room, this day, and arrive somewhere else entirely.

Imagine you are standing at the edge of a forest. The light is golden and soft — afternoon sun filtering through the canopy of tall trees. You step in.

The ground beneath your feet is soft with moss and earth. With every step, you feel the forest absorb any weight you were carrying. The air here is cool, clean, and fragrant — pine, earth, and something sweet you can't quite name.

You walk slowly, with no destination. The sounds of the world you came from have faded. Here, there is only birdsong, the rustle of leaves in a gentle wind, and the sound of a small stream somewhere nearby.

You find a clearing. A patch of warm sunlight falls here like a gift. You lie down on the soft earth. Above you, the branches form a cathedral. You watch the light move and dance.

You belong here. The forest knows you. You take a long, slow breath of this forest air... and exhale everything you don't need.

Stay here. Rest here. There is nothing to do, nowhere to be. You are completely safe and completely at peace.

(pause 30 seconds)

When you're ready, begin to feel the surface beneath you returning — your mat, this room. Carry a fragment of that forest with you as you return."

## SECTION 12: TEACHING LANGUAGE & CUES

### Opening a Class — Setting Intention and Inviting Presence

**Arriving Cues:**
- "Find a comfortable position — seated, lying down, or any way your body wants to land today."
- "Close your eyes, or let your gaze soften downward."
- "Let this be the one place, this hour, where you don't need to be anywhere else or anyone else."
- "Take a full breath in through the nose... and let it go completely. Notice the exhale is always longer than you expect."
- "Feel where you are touching the mat. Let the mat hold you."

**Intention Setting:**
- "Before we begin, I invite you to set an intention for your practice today. Not a goal, not an achievement — simply a quality you want to cultivate. Perhaps it's presence. Perhaps it's softness. Perhaps it's simply showing up."
- "Let this intention be like a seed you plant at the beginning of class. We'll return to it at the end, to see how it's grown."
- "Your intention is yours alone. Hold it quietly, like something precious."

---

### Encouraging Phrases During Practice

**General encouragement:**
- "You're doing beautifully."
- "Trust what your body is telling you."
- "There is no 'perfect' pose — there is only your pose, right now."
- "Every breath is an opportunity to let go a little more."
- "If you fall out of balance, you simply get to begin again. That IS the practice."
- "The pose is just the container. The breath is the practice."
- "Notice the difference between discomfort and pain. Discomfort is a teacher. Pain is a signal."

**Building heat:**
- "Breathe through it. Let the breath be bigger than the challenge."
- "You are stronger than you think right now."
- "One more breath. You've got this."
- "Feel the heat — that's transformation happening."
- "Stay. Stay. Stay."

**Cooling down:**
- "You've done the work. Now let the body receive."
- "Surrender. Release. Trust."
- "Let the earth hold you — you don't have to hold yourself right now."
- "Soften the grip. Soften the jaw. Soften the effort."
- "The body knows how to release. Just stop interfering."

---

### Offering Modifications Kindly

**Framing principles:**
- Always offer modifications BEFORE the full pose (not as a remediation after failure).
- Use language like "one option," "a wonderful choice," not "if you can't do this."
- Celebrate modifications as intelligent practice.

**Phrases:**
- "One option here — and this is a complete, full expression of the pose — is to..."
- "If your body is saying 'not today' to this, listen to that wisdom. Instead, try..."
- "There's no hierarchy here. The most advanced practice is the one that honors what your body needs."
- "We can make this pose work for you, not the other way around."
- "Using a block here isn't a crutch — it's bringing the floor to you."
- "If your hamstrings would like a little more room to breathe, soften the knee."
- "This variation will give you all the benefits — and maybe even more — than the full pose."

---

### Transitions Between Poses (Linking Language)

**Moving forward:**
- "And on your next exhale..."
- "When you're ready, begin to..."
- "Slowly and with intention..."
- "Let the breath guide you as you..."
- "Take one more breath here. And on the exhale, begin to..."

**Side transitions:**
- "Now let's bring the same quality of attention to the other side."
- "Release, and find your way back to center. Then we'll explore the left."
- "Notice any difference between the two sides — there's wisdom there."

**Returning to neutral:**
- "Come back to a neutral spine."
- "Return to Tadasana — Mountain Pose — and feel the effects of that posture."
- "Rest for a moment in Child's Pose, letting the body integrate."
- "Make your way to Downward Dog — our home base between sequences."

---

### Closing a Class — Namaste and Closing Words

**Preparing for Savasana:**
- "You have done something profound today — you showed up for yourself. Now let the body receive the full benefit of that work."
- "Transition now to Savasana. Allow every effort to dissolve. This is your final pose — some say the most important pose in all of yoga."

**Closing words (before Namaste):**
- "As you return to sitting, carry with you the stillness of this practice. Let the outside world wait a few more moments."
- "Notice how you feel right now — compared to how you arrived. That change? That is yours. You made that."
- "The light in me recognizes and honors the light in you."
- "We end as we began — together, and present."

**Namaste gesture:**
- "Bring the hands to heart center. Bow the head, closing the eyes."
- *Spoken:* "Namaste." *(The teacher speaks it first, students echo.)*
- "Namaste: I bow to the divine in you. Thank you for sharing your practice with me today."

---

### Building Heat vs. Cooling Down — Language and Tone

**Building Heat (Warm-Up → Peak):**
- Voice: stronger, steady, rhythmic, like a drumbeat
- Pace: synchronized with breath — every word lands with a breath
- Silence: use less silence — keep the energy moving
- Cues: action-oriented — "Press," "lift," "reach," "engage," "rise"
- Energy: building, ascending, forward

**Example:** *"Exhale, plant the hands. Inhale, step back to plank — strong through the core. Exhale, lower Chaturanga — elbows back. Inhale, rise through Upward Dog — chest forward, shoulders back. Exhale, Down Dog. Five breaths — stay with me."*

**Cooling Down (Cool-Down → Savasana):**
- Voice: lower in pitch, softer, slower, like water
- Pace: spacious — leave silence for sensation
- Silence: use generous silence — let students feel the poses working
- Cues: receptive — "release," "soften," "melt," "allow," "surrender," "let go"
- Energy: descending, inward, quiet

**Example:** *"Let the knees fall open... find the earth beneath you... with each exhale, allow the body to become a little heavier... a little more still... there is nothing to do here... simply be."*

## SECTION 13: YOGA PHILOSOPHY

### The 8 Limbs of Yoga (Ashtanga — Patanjali's Yoga Sutras)

The 8 Limbs form a complete path toward liberation (Samadhi). They are sequential but also simultaneous — like spokes of a wheel.

---

**1. Yama — Social Ethics (External Restraints)**
*German: Soziale Ethik / Äußere Disziplinen*

The five Yamas are guidelines for how we treat others and the world:
- **Ahimsa** — Non-violence: in action, speech, and thought
- **Satya** — Truthfulness: aligned word, thought, and deed
- **Asteya** — Non-stealing: not taking what isn't freely given (including time, energy, credit)
- **Brahmacharya** — Moderation / right use of energy: originally celibacy; modernly, conserving vital energy
- **Aparigraha** — Non-grasping / non-greed: letting go of possessiveness and attachment

---

**2. Niyama — Personal Practices (Internal Observances)**
*German: Persönliche Disziplinen / Innere Observanz*

Guidelines for how we treat ourselves:
- **Saucha** — Cleanliness: of body, mind, environment, speech
- **Santosha** — Contentment: finding sufficiency in what is
- **Tapas** — Disciplined effort: the heat of transformation through committed practice
- **Svadhyaya** — Self-study: study of sacred texts AND observation of the self
- **Ishvara Pranidhana** — Surrender to the divine / a higher principle

---

**3. Asana — Posture**
*German: Körperhaltung*

In Patanjali's original text, Asana is defined as "a seat that is both stable and comfortable" (Sthira Sukham Asanam). It referred to meditation seat — not 84,000 poses. The purpose of physical practice is to prepare the body to sit still for meditation.

---

**4. Pranayama — Breath Extension**
*German: Atemübungen / Atemregulation*

Control and extension of the life force (Prana) through breath. When the breath is steady, the mind becomes steady. Pranayama is the bridge between body and mind.

---

**5. Pratyahara — Withdrawal of the Senses**
*German: Sinnesrückzug*

Turning attention inward, away from external stimuli. In yoga class, this happens naturally in Savasana — when the lights dim, eyes close, and sound fades. We stop reacting to the outside world and begin to witness the inner world.

---

**6. Dharana — Concentration**
*German: Konzentration*

Fixing the mind on a single point — a flame, a mantra, the breath, a sensation in the body. The practice of Dharana trains the mind to stay. This is what we practice every time we return to the breath after distraction.

---

**7. Dhyana — Meditation**
*German: Meditation*

Uninterrupted concentration — where Dharana flows into effortless attention. The observer and the observed begin to merge. Time dissolves. This cannot be forced; it arises when conditions are right.

---

**8. Samadhi — Integration / Bliss / Liberation**
*German: Erleuchtung / Integration / Glückseligkeit*

The final limb — a state of complete union between individual consciousness and universal consciousness. Not a permanent state in most human experience, but moments of profound stillness, clarity, and oneness. What brings us back to the mat, again and again.

---

### Key Philosophical Concepts

**Ahimsa — Non-Violence**
*German: Gewaltlosigkeit*
The first and most foundational ethical principle. Begins with non-violence toward the self: not pushing past pain, not speaking harshly to yourself when you fall, not comparing yourself to others on the mat. *"The most advanced yogi in the room is often the one resting in Child's Pose."*

**Satya — Truthfulness**
*German: Wahrhaftigkeit*
The integrity of practice. Honoring where your body actually is today — not where you wish it were or where it was last week. Truth in yoga is listening deeply and responding honestly.

**Santosha — Contentment**
*German: Zufriedenheit*
The radical acceptance of what is. Not passive resignation, but a profound recognition that this moment, this breath, this imperfect practice — it is enough. One of the greatest antidotes to the "always more, always better" culture.

**Tapas — Disciplined Effort / Transformative Heat**
*German: Disziplinierte Bemühung / Transformative Hitze*
The willingness to do the work even when it's uncomfortable. In yoga, Tapas is what brings you to the mat on the days you don't want to come. It's the heat generated by commitment. Without Tapas, transformation is theoretical.

**Svadhyaya — Self-Study**
*German: Selbststudium*
Two-pronged: study of sacred texts (which hold wisdom beyond our personal experience) and honest self-observation. On the mat: noticing your habitual reactions, your patterns, where you grip, where you avoid. The mat as mirror.

---

### Weaving Philosophy into a Modern Yoga Class (Without Being Preachy)

**Principles:**
1. **One theme per class** — don't lecture. Plant one seed and trust it to grow.
2. **Embody the principle** — teach Ahimsa by the way you offer modifications, not by explaining it.
3. **Ask questions, don't give answers** — "Is this Tapas, or is this pushing past wisdom? Only you know the answer."
4. **Use personal stories sparingly** — a brief, honest story is worth more than a long sermon.
5. **Let the practice teach** — often the body, the breath, the silence of Savasana will communicate what no words can.

**Weaving examples:**
- *Opening (Santosha):* "Whatever state you arrived in today — distracted, tired, energized — this is your practice. There's no version of you that's more worthy of being here."
- *Standing poses (Tapas):* "Stay one more breath. This is Tapas — the willingness to remain in the fire long enough to be changed by it."
- *Modifications (Ahimsa):* "Honor your body's intelligence today. Ahimsa — non-harm — begins here, on your mat, with yourself."
- *Savasana (Pratyahara):* "Turn your senses inward. Let the outside world wait. This time belongs to you."

---

### Sankalpa — Setting Intention
*German: Absichtssetzung / Herzensausrichtung*

**What it is:**
Sankalpa is a Sanskrit word meaning "intention" or "resolve." San = connection to the highest truth; Kalpa = vow. It's a short, positive, present-tense statement of the heart's deepest intention.

**Not the same as a goal** — a goal is future-oriented and result-driven. A Sankalpa is already true at the level of the soul; we are simply aligning with it.

**Examples:**
- "I am open to what is."
- "I am at peace."
- "I have enough. I am enough."
- "I meet challenges with grace."

**How to teach it:**
- *"Find your intention — not a goal or a wish, but a quality of being. Perhaps a single word: ease, presence, strength, love. Let it arise naturally."*
- *"Plant this intention at the beginning of practice. Return to it in the final pose. Notice where the practice has watered that seed."*
- Used most powerfully in Yoga Nidra but beautiful in every class.

## SECTION 14: ANATOMY HIGHLIGHTS FOR TEACHING

### Hip Flexors: Psoas and Iliacus

**The Psoas Major:**
- Runs from T12–L5 vertebrae (lumbar spine) → through the pelvis → attaches to the lesser trochanter of the femur
- The only muscle connecting the spine to the leg — a profound structural role
- Called the "muscle of the soul" in some traditions (it houses fight-or-flight activation)

**The Iliacus:**
- Lines the inner bowl of the pelvis → attaches alongside the psoas at the femur
- Together they form the Iliopsoas — the primary hip flexor

**Why they matter in yoga:**
- Modern humans sit for 8–12 hours/day — the psoas chronically shortens and tightens
- Tight psoas → anterior pelvic tilt → lower back pain (very common presenting complaint)
- Tight psoas inhibits full hip extension in Warrior I, Crescent Lunge, Dhanurasana
- Emotional holding: the psoas is the first muscle to contract in fear; chronic stress = chronic psoas tension

**How to cue psoas release:**
- In Low Lunge: *"Rather than pressing the back knee into the floor, feel as if the front hip bone is moving forward and down. Feel the deep release in the front of the back hip."*
- In Pigeon: *"The release you're feeling deep in the back hip — that's the psoas beginning to let go. Breathe into it."*

---

### Hamstrings: Role in Forward Folds and Injury Prevention

**Anatomy:**
Three muscles: Biceps Femoris, Semitendinosus, Semimembranosus — all cross the hip and knee joint.

**Key Function in Yoga:**
- They lengthen in forward folds (hip flexion with straight knee)
- They stabilize the knee in standing poses
- They control the eccentric lowering in Chaturanga (assisted by the glutes)

**Why they're often "tight":**
- Often it's not the muscle itself but the sciatic nerve being restricted by surrounding fascia
- Sitting shortens the muscle; neural tension can limit the sensation of "reach"

**Injury prevention:**
- Most hamstring strains happen at the proximal attachment (ischial tuberosity — "sitting bone" insertion)
- Risk is highest in cold muscles combined with sudden deep forward folds
- **The rule:** always warm the hamstrings before deep forward folds (Sun Salutations + low lunges first)

**Teaching cues:**
- *"Soften the knee if you feel a pulling sensation behind the knee or in the lower back — that's neural tension, not hamstring flexibility."*
- *"The hip crease folds, not the waist. Imagine the crease at the top of your thigh — that's your hinge."*
- *"Micro-bend the knee to protect the hamstring insertion at the sitting bone."*

---

### Thoracic Spine: Why Backbends Feel Hard

**Anatomy:**
12 thoracic vertebrae (T1–T12), attached to ribs on both sides. Naturally has a mild kyphosis (convex curve = rounding).

**Modern problem:**
- Prolonged sitting → forward head posture + rounded upper back (increased thoracic kyphosis)
- The thoracic spine stiffens into flexion — extension (backbending) becomes difficult
- When thoracic mobility is lacking, the body compensates by over-extending the lumbar spine (dangerous) or the cervical spine (neck strain)

**Why this matters for backbends:**
- In Camel, Wheel, and Upward Dog — the goal is thoracic extension, not lumbar crunch
- Students feel "nothing" in the upper back and "everything" in the lower back → need to mobilize T-spine first

**Preparation poses:**
- Thoracic rolls (foam roller or ball between shoulder blades)
- Gomukhasana arms (shoulder external rotation)
- Cat/Cow with emphasis on thoracic
- Gentle Sphinx Pose with hands closer (encourages T-spine extension)

**Teaching cues:**
- *"Instead of crunching into the lower back, imagine trying to create space between each rib. Lift the sternum."*
- *"Shoulder blades come together and slide down the back — this action opens the thoracic spine."*

---

### Shoulder Girdle: Alignment in Down Dog and Chaturanga

**Key Structures:**
- Glenohumeral joint (ball and socket) — highly mobile, therefore vulnerable
- Rotator cuff (SITS: Supraspinatus, Infraspinatus, Teres Minor, Subscapularis) — provides stability
- Scapulae — their movement (protraction/retraction, elevation/depression) fundamentally affects shoulder health

**Downward Dog alignment:**
- Hands shoulder-width, fingers spread (equal pressure through all points of the hand)
- External rotation of the upper arm: spin the inner elbows toward each other (biceps facing the thumbs)
- Shoulder blades broad across the back — neither pinching together nor dramatically lifting (serratus anterior engages)
- Ears between the upper arms (not lower — avoid shoulder shrugging)

**Chaturanga alignment (the most injury-risky pose in yoga):**
- Elbows at 90° — no deeper
- Elbows track over the wrists and back (not splaying)
- Shoulders NO LOWER than elbows — the most critical rule (prevents rotator cuff impingement)
- Core deeply engaged — the entire body is a plank as it lowers
- *Common mistake:* Collapsing the chest down while the elbows splay = direct path to shoulder injury

**Teaching cue for Chaturanga:**
- *"Imagine you're wearing a straight jacket — elbows hug into the ribs as you lower. The body moves as one unit, like a plank of wood."*
- *"Shoulders stay at elbow height or slightly above — never below. If you can't hold this, come to the knees."*

---

### Core Engagement: Bandhas

**Mula Bandha — Root Lock**
*German: Wurzelverschluss*

**What it is:** A subtle contraction of the pelvic floor — the muscular diaphragm at the base of the pelvis. Not squeezing the buttocks (external) — more like gently lifting the perineum upward from within.

**How to find it:** Imagine stopping the flow of urine mid-stream. Then release 50% of that effort — this lighter engagement is Mula Bandha.

**Effects:**
- Grounds energy (Apana Vayu) upward
- Stabilizes the pelvis and lower spine
- Activates the deep core (transversus abdominis)
- Supports the lower back in all weight-bearing poses

**Teaching cue:** *"From the pelvic floor, draw lightly upward — as if you're gently lifting something from within. This is subtle but powerful — the foundation of your entire practice."*

---

**Uddiyana Bandha — Upward Flying Lock**
*German: Aufwärtsverschluss*

**What it is:** A drawing in and upward of the lower belly — the space between the navel and pubic bone. The naval draws back and up toward the spine.

**How to find it:** On an exhale, let the belly hollow naturally. Then maintain a fraction of that hollowing as you inhale.

**Effects:**
- Protects the lower back in all spinal loading
- Lifts the prana upward (energizing)
- Supports arm balances — essential for inversions and arm balances
- Engages the transversus abdominis (deepest core layer)

**Teaching cue:** *"Draw your lower belly gently in and up — as if you're creating a small hammock of support just below the navel. Don't hold the breath — keep breathing, but maintain the inner lift."*

---

**Jalandhara Bandha — Throat Lock**
*German: Kehlensperre*

Chin slightly lowered toward the chest, elongating the back of the neck. Used primarily in pranayama and seated meditation.
