## The 8 Limbs of Yoga (Ashtanga — Patanjali's Yoga Sutras)

Patanjali codified yoga as an eight-limbed path around 400 CE. The physical postures (Asana) are only one of the eight limbs — yoga is fundamentally a path of mind and consciousness.

### 1. Yama — Ethical Guidelines (How we treat the world)
The five Yamas are social restraints, the foundation of practice.
- **Ahimsa** (Non-violence): Non-harming in thought, word, and action — toward others AND oneself. In practice: listen to the body, don't force.
- **Satya** (Truthfulness): Being honest with ourselves about what we feel in a pose, in our lives.
- **Asteya** (Non-stealing): Not comparing ourselves to others on the mat. Your practice is your own.
- **Brahmacharya** (Right use of energy): Directing our vitality wisely. Not exhausting ourselves or holding back unnecessarily.
- **Aparigraha** (Non-grasping): Not clinging to outcomes. Letting go of what a pose "should" look like.

### 2. Niyama — Personal Disciplines (How we treat ourselves)
- **Saucha** (Cleanliness/Purity): Purifying the body and mind through practice.
- **Santosha** (Contentment): Being at peace with where we are today. This is the antidote to "I'm not flexible enough."
- **Tapas** (Discipline/Heat): The willingness to show up even when it's hard. "Do the practice and all is coming." (Pattabhi Jois)
- **Svadhyaya** (Self-study): Observing ourselves without judgment. The mat is a mirror.
- **Ishvara Pranidhana** (Surrender): Releasing the need to control outcomes. Trusting the process.

### 3. Asana — Physical Postures
The poses we practice to make the body a fit vehicle for meditation. The goal is a body that can sit comfortably for long periods, and a mind that can settle.

### 4. Pranayama — Breath Extension
Conscious regulation of the breath. Prana means life force; Ayama means extension. The breath is the bridge between body and mind. "The quality of our breathing reflects the quality of our mind."

### 5. Pratyahara — Withdrawal of the Senses
Turning attention inward. In Savasana, we practice Pratyahara — the outside world recedes. Each time we close our eyes in a pose, we practice it.

### 6. Dharana — Concentration
Focusing the mind on a single object (the breath, a mantra, a point on the body). Develops into meditation.

### 7. Dhyana — Meditation
Sustained, effortless concentration. The mind flows continuously toward its object. Not forced — it arises from consistent Dharana practice.

### 8. Samadhi — Integration / Bliss
The state where the distinction between the meditator and the object of meditation dissolves. Described as pure awareness, union. This is the "Yoga" — the union.

## Key Concepts for Class

### Ahimsa in Practice
**How to weave it in:** "Ahimsa — non-violence — begins on your own mat. If something hurts, that's your teacher asking you to back off. Pain is not the goal. We work at the edge of sensation, not beyond it."

### Santosha (Contentment) in Practice
**How to weave it in:** "Santosha — contentment — is the practice of recognizing that this moment, exactly as it is, is enough. The pose you can do today is exactly the right pose for today."

### Sankalpa — Intention Setting
A Sankalpa is a heartfelt intention or resolve. Unlike a goal (which is future-oriented), a Sankalpa is a statement of who you already are, or who you are becoming.

**How to guide intention setting at the opening of class:**
> "Take a moment to close your eyes and arrive in this space. Take a deep breath. As you exhale, let go of anything you carried in with you from the day. Ask yourself — what do you need from this practice today? It might be a word: strength, softness, presence, joy. Let it arise naturally, without forcing it. Hold it lightly as we begin."

**Returning to intention mid-class:**
> "Check in with your intention — does it still feel true? Can you feel it somewhere in the body?"

**Closing with intention:**
> "As you come to rest, return to the intention you set at the beginning. Notice how it has evolved. You carry it with you as you move back into your day."

### Tapas — Showing Up
**How to weave it in:** "There will be days when the last thing you want to do is come to the mat. That impulse to stay anyway — that's Tapas. The discipline is not in how hard you push; it's in the showing up."

### Svadhyaya — The Mat as Mirror
**How to weave it in:** "Notice what this pose brings up for you. Is there frustration? Impatience? Ease? Judgment? Whatever arises is information — you don't need to fix it. Just observe."

### Prana — Life Force
**How to weave it in:** "Prana is the animating force in all living things. Every inhale is an invitation of prana into the body. Every exhale is a release. With each breath, we participate in the great rhythm of life."

## Yoga Philosophy in a Modern Class — Principles

1. **Don't lecture.** Offer one philosophical seed per class, briefly. Let students sit with it.
2. **Make it personal and relatable.** Connect philosophy to what students are feeling in their bodies right now.
3. **Use the pose as metaphor.** Warrior I: "What does it mean to hold your ground with an open heart?"
4. **Invite inquiry, not answers.** "Notice…" "Ask yourself…" "What if…" — not "you must feel…"
5. **Return to the body.** Philosophy becomes meaningful when it is felt, not just understood.
6. **Keep it universal.** Avoid heavy Sanskrit jargon unless you explain it. Keep it accessible.
7. **Close with gratitude.** The tradition of "Namaste" (the light in me bows to the light in you) creates genuine connection.

## The Concept of Drishti (Gazing Points)

In yoga, the Drishti is the focal point for the gaze in each pose. It stabilizes balance, focuses the mind, and cultivates Dharana (concentration).

Key Drishtis:
- **Nasagrai** — tip of the nose (forward folds)
- **Urdhva** — up toward the sky (Warrior I, arms overhead)
- **Hastagrai** — tip of the middle finger (Extended Side Angle)
- **Padhayoragrai** — toes (seated forward folds)
- **Nabi** — navel (Down Dog)
- **Bhrumadhya** — third eye, between the eyebrows (closing meditation)
- A fixed point in space — any pose requiring balance

**Teaching cue:** "Find your Drishti — a soft, still gaze. Your eyes and your mind settle together."

## Namaste & Closing a Class

The gesture of Anjali Mudra (hands in prayer at the heart) with the bow and the word "Namaste" closes the practice. It acknowledges the shared humanity and inner light of every person in the room.

**Sample closing words:**
> "Bring the hands to the heart. Take a moment to honor the effort you brought to your mat today. The practice asks something of us every time — and every time we show up, we grow, even if we can't see it yet. The light in me recognizes and honors the light in you. Namaste."
