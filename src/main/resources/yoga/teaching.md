# Yoga Teaching Language Reference

The language a yoga teacher uses is as important as the poses. These are practical scripts, phrases, and principles for every phase of class.

---

## Opening a Class

### Arriving & Centering
> "Find a comfortable position — seated or lying down. Take a moment to arrive. Let the eyes close. Begin to notice the breath, without changing anything. Just arrive."

> "Take a breath in through the nose... and a long exhale through the mouth. Again — inhale... and release. One more — really let that exhale carry away anything you no longer need from the day."

> "You made it. Whatever it took to get you here today — that took something. Honor that."

### Setting an Intention (Sankalpa)
> "Before we begin, I invite you to set an intention for your practice today. Not a goal, not an achievement — simply a quality you want to cultivate. Perhaps it's presence. Softness. Courage. Ease. Let whatever needs to arise come forward."

> "Hold your intention quietly, like something precious. We'll return to it at the end."

> "Your intention is yours alone. It doesn't need to make sense to anyone else."

## Encouraging Phrases — During Practice

### General (use throughout)
- "You're exactly where you need to be."
- "There is no perfect pose — there is only your pose, right now."
- "Every breath is an opportunity to let go a little more."
- "The pose is just the container. The breath is the practice."
- "Notice the difference between discomfort and pain. Discomfort is a teacher. Pain is a signal."
- "If you fall out of balance, you get to begin again. That IS the practice."
- "Trust what your body is telling you."

### Building Heat (Warm-up → Peak)
Use these when the energy is rising. Voice: stronger, rhythmic, forward-moving.
- "Breathe through it. Let the breath be bigger than the challenge."
- "You are stronger than you think right now."
- "One more breath. You've got this."
- "Feel the heat — that's transformation happening."
- "Stay. Stay. Stay."
- "Press into the floor — feel the floor press back."
- "Find your edge and breathe there."

**Tone & pace example:**
> *"Exhale, plant the hands. Inhale, step back to plank — strong through the core. Exhale, lower Chaturanga — elbows in. Inhale, rise through Upward Dog — chest forward, shoulders back. Exhale, Down Dog. Five breaths — stay with me."*

### Cooling Down
Use these from cool-down onward. Voice: lower, softer, slower.
- "You've done the work. Now let the body receive."
- "Surrender. Release. Trust."
- "Let the earth hold you — you don't have to hold yourself right now."
- "Soften the grip. Soften the jaw. Soften the effort."
- "The body knows how to release. Just stop interfering."
- "Every exhale, a little heavier. A little more still."

**Tone & pace example:**
> *"Let the knees fall open... find the earth beneath you... with each exhale, allow the body to become a little heavier... a little more still... there is nothing to do here... simply be."*

## Offering Modifications

**Key principle:** Always offer the modification BEFORE the full pose, not as a fix for failure. Celebrate it as intelligent practice.

### Framing language
- "One option here — and this is a complete expression of the pose — is to..."
- "If your body is saying 'not today' to this, listen to that wisdom."
- "There's no hierarchy here. The most advanced practice is the one that honors what your body needs."
- "We can make this pose work for you — not the other way around."
- "Using a block here isn't a crutch — it's bringing the floor to you."
- "This variation will give you every benefit of the full pose — and maybe more."
- "If your hamstrings are asking for a little more room, soften the knee."

### Specific modifiers
- **For hamstring tightness:** "Bend the knees generously."
- **For wrist sensitivity:** "Try fists on the mat, or come to the forearms."
- **For lower back:** "Widen the stance. Shorten the depth."
- **For tight hips in Pigeon:** "Try Reclined Pigeon — same benefit, much more accessible."
- **For Chaturanga:** "Lower the knees — this builds the same strength with less strain."

## Transitions Between Poses (Linking Language)

### Moving into a pose
- "And on your next exhale..."
- "When you're ready, begin to..."
- "Slowly and with intention..."
- "Let the breath guide you as you..."
- "Take one more breath here. And on the exhale, begin to..."
- "From here, we'll make our way into..."

### Changing sides
- "Now let's bring the same quality of attention to the other side."
- "Release, find your way back to center, then we'll explore the left."
- "Notice any difference between the two sides — there's wisdom there."

### Returning to neutral
- "Come back to a neutral spine."
- "Return to Tadasana — Mountain Pose — and feel the effects."
- "Rest in Child's Pose, letting the body integrate."
- "Make your way to Down Dog — our home base between sequences."

### Vinyasa cue (the linking breath)
- "Take a Vinyasa if you'd like — or come directly to Down Dog. Both are perfect."
- "Flow through at your own pace — Plank, Chaturanga, Up Dog, Down Dog."
- "Or simply lower the knees, shift back to Child's Pose — rest is always available."

## Phase-Specific Tone

| Phase | Voice quality | Pace | Silence | Cue style |
|-------|--------------|------|---------|-----------|
| Centering | Soft, warm | Slow | Generous | "Notice… allow…" |
| Warm-up | Gentle, inviting | Medium | Moderate | "Begin to… explore…" |
| Building | Steady, rhythmic | With breath | Brief | "Press… lift… reach…" |
| Peak | Strong, supportive | Breath-driven | Minimal | "Stay… breathe… hold…" |
| Cool-down | Lower, softer | Slow | Generous | "Release… melt… soften…" |
| Savasana | Near-whisper | Very slow | Long pauses | "Let go… surrender… rest…" |

## Closing a Class

### Preparing for Savasana
> "You have done something profound today — you showed up for yourself. Now let the body receive the full benefit of that work."

> "Transition to Savasana. Let every effort dissolve. This is your final pose — some say the most important pose in all of yoga."

### After Savasana — returning
> "Begin to deepen the breath. Let the body know rest is complete. Gently wiggle the fingers and toes. Roll to one side — take a moment there. And slowly, in your own time, return to sitting."

### Closing words (before Namaste)
> "Notice how you feel right now — compared to how you arrived. That change? That is yours. You made that."

> "As you return to your day, carry some of this stillness with you. It doesn't have to disappear at the door."

> "The practice is not what happened on the mat. The practice is who you are when you step off it."

> "We end as we began — together, and present."

### Namaste
- Bring the hands to heart center (Anjali Mudra). Bow the head slightly, closing the eyes.
- Teacher speaks *"Namaste"* first. Students echo.
- Optional: *"Namaste — I honor the light in you. Thank you for sharing your practice with me today."*

## Addressing Common Moments

### When students are struggling in a hard pose
> "This is where the practice lives — right here at the edge. Take one more breath. You can always back off. And you can always stay."

### When the room goes quiet and tense
> "Check in with the breath. If you've lost it, that's your sign to ease back. The breath is always the priority."

### When someone falls out of a balance pose
> *Smile or acknowledge warmly:* "There it is — the moment of learning. Come right back."

### When the energy is low
> "Notice the quality of your energy right now — honor it. Some days the practice is fire. Some days it's water. Both are yoga."

### When something is emotionally releasing (e.g., deep hip openers)
> "If something unexpected arises here — a feeling, an emotion — that's the practice working. You're in a safe space. Stay with the breath."

## Cue Construction Principles

1. **Lead with the breath.** "Inhale as you..." or "On your exhale..." comes before the action.
2. **Prioritize alignment over aesthetics.** Cue function, not appearance: "Root through the four corners of your foot" not "make the foot flat."
3. **Less is more.** Give 2–3 alignment cues per pose, not 10. Let students feel.
4. **Invite, don't command.** "You might try..." "Notice if..." "See what happens when..." rather than "You must..."
5. **Use sensation language.** "Feel the stretch in the back thigh." "Notice the lift in the chest."
6. **Name the benefit occasionally.** "This releases the psoas — your hip flexor — which gets shortened from sitting all day."
7. **Silence is a cue.** Silence after a cue lets the cue land. Don't fill every moment with words.
